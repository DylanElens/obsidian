           to them. Find the criterion with the biggest 
           difference that matters, and give that 
           criterion a swing weight of 100.

           Second, assign points to relative value of ‘swing’

           standard. The facilitator looks at each criterion in
           turn and compares its swing in value with the value

           80% as much value, going from 0 to 100, as the
           standard, then it would be given a weight of 80.

           flip chart or the whiteboard.

Weighted Sum Model:

The Weighted Sum Model is a straightforward approach to MCDA where each 
stakeholder assigns weights to the criteria based on their preferences and priorities. 
These weights reflect the relative importance of each criterion in the decision-making process.
The overall score for each alternative is then calculated as a weighted sum
of its performance on each criterion, using the weights provided by the stakeholders.

highest score is considered the most preferable.

Example: Weighted Sum Model in Choosing a Car:

Suppose a group of stakeholders is trying to choose the best car for a company fleet. 
They have identified three criteria as important for their decision: fuel efficiency, safety 
rating, and purchase price. Three stakeholders with different preferences are involved:

Stakeholder A: Places high importance on fuel efficiency (50%), 
                moderate importance on safety rating (30%), and low
                importance on purchase price (20%).
Stakeholder B: Places moderate importance on fuel efficiency (30%), 
                high importance on safety rating (50%), and moderate
                importance on purchase price (20%).
Stakeholder C: Places low importance on fuel efficiency (20%), low importance
                on safety rating (20%), and high importance on purchase price (60%).

Now, they have three car alternatives:

Car X: Fuel efficiency score of 40, safety rating of 90, purchase price of $25,000.
Car Y: Fuel efficiency score of 35, safety rating of 85, purchase price of $20,000.
Car Z: Fuel efficiency score of 50, safety rating of 92, purchase price of $30,000.
Using the Weighted Sum Model, we calculate the overall scores for each car for each stakeholder:

For Stakeholder A:

Car X: (0.50 * 40) + (0.30 * 90) + (0.20 * 25,000) = 50 + 27 + 5,000 = 5,077
Car Y: (0.50 * 35) + (0.30 * 85) + (0.20 * 20,000) = 5,070
Car Z: (0.50 * 50) + (0.30 * 92) + (0.20 * 30,000) = 6,100
For Stakeholder B and C, similar calculations are made.

Based on these scores, each stakeholder will have a different preference order 
for the cars. Stakeholder A might prefer Car Z, while Stakeholder B might prefer 
Car X, and Stakeholder C might prefer Car Y. The final decision may involve discussions 
and negotiations among stakeholders to reach a consensus or a compromise.
